{{< bp >}}
Now Caesar had more than suspicions. Clear facts had been added to them.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
{{< name "Dumnorix" "DUM-noh-rix" >}} had led the {{< name "Helvetii" "hel-VEE-shee-eye" >}} through the land of the {{< name "Sequani" "seh-KWAH-nee" >}}. He had arranged for both sides to give hostages. He had done these things without Caesar's orders, without orders from his own state, and even without the {{< name "Aedui" "EYE-dyoo-eye" >}} knowing about it.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
The chief magistrate of the Aedui had also rebuked him. Caesar believed there was enough reason either to punish Dumnorix himself or to order the Aeduan state to punish him.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
But one thing held Caesar back: Dumnorix was the brother of {{< name "Divitiacus" "dih-vih-tee-AH-kus" >}}.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar had learned by experience that Divitiacus had great loyalty toward the Roman people. Caesar also respected his affection, faithfulness, justice, and moderation. He feared that punishing Dumnorix would deeply hurt Divitiacus.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
So before doing anything, Caesar summoned Divitiacus. He sent away the usual interpreters and spoke with him through {{< name "Caius Valerius Procillus" "KY-us vah-LEER-ee-us proh-KIL-us" >}}, a leading man of the Roman Province in Gaul. Caesar trusted Procillus greatly.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar reminded Divitiacus of what had been said about Dumnorix in the council of the Gauls, where Divitiacus himself had been present. He also told him what other people had said privately in Caesar's presence.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Then Caesar asked Divitiacus not to be offended. Caesar wanted either to judge Dumnorix himself after trying the case, or else to order the Aeduan state to do it.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Divitiacus embraced Caesar and began to beg him, with many tears, not to pass a very harsh sentence on his brother.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Divitiacus said he knew the charges were true. No one, he said, felt more pain over them than he did. When Divitiacus had great influence at home and in the rest of Gaul, Dumnorix had been young and had little power. Dumnorix had grown strong through Divitiacus's help.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Now, Divitiacus said, Dumnorix was using that power not only to lessen Divitiacus's popularity, but almost to ruin him. Still, Divitiacus was moved by brotherly love and by what people would think.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
If Caesar punished Dumnorix severely, everyone would believe Divitiacus had agreed to it, because Divitiacus was so close to Caesar. Then, he feared, all Gaul would turn against him in their hearts.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Divitiacus kept pleading with tears. Caesar took his right hand and comforted him. He asked him to stop begging.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar said his regard for Divitiacus was so great that, because of his wishes and prayers, Caesar would forgive both the wrongs done to the republic and the private wrongs done to himself.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Then Caesar summoned Dumnorix and brought in Divitiacus too. Caesar explained what he blamed Dumnorix for. He laid out what he himself had learned and what the Aeduan state complained about.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar warned Dumnorix to avoid every reason for suspicion in the future. He said he was pardoning the past for the sake of Dumnorix's brother, Divitiacus.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
But Caesar did not simply forget the matter. He placed watchers over Dumnorix, so he could know what Dumnorix did and whom he talked with.
{{< /bp >}}
