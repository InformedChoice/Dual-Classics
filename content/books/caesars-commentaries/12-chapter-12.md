{{< bp >}}
Before the battle began, Caesar sent away his own horse and then ordered the other officers' horses removed too. He wanted the danger to be the same for everyone. If there were no horses nearby, no one could hope to ride away from the fight.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Then Caesar encouraged his men, and the battle began.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
The Roman soldiers were on higher ground. They threw their javelins down into the {{< name "Helvetii" "hel-VEE-shee-eye" >}} and broke up the enemy's phalanx. Then, when the tight formation was scattered, the Romans drew their swords and charged.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
The Roman javelins made fighting hard for the Gauls. A javelin could pierce through several shields at once and fasten them together. Sometimes the iron point bent, so the weapon could not easily be pulled out.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
When this happened, a warrior's left arm could be trapped by his own shield. Many struggled for a long time, shaking their arms, but at last threw away their shields and fought without protection.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
After many wounds and much fighting, the Helvetii began to give ground. There was another mountain about a mile away, and they retreated toward it.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
When they reached that mountain, the Romans advanced after them. But then about fifteen thousand men of the {{< name "Boii" "BOY-eye" >}} and {{< name "Tulingi" "too-LING-ee" >}} attacked the Romans on their exposed side. These men had been guarding the rear of the Helvetian line.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
When the Helvetii on the mountain saw this, they began pressing forward again and renewed the battle.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
The Romans turned to face both dangers. Their first and second lines fought the Helvetii who had already been driven back. Their third line turned against the Boii and Tulingi who were just arriving.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
The fight was long and fierce. For a while, the outcome was uncertain.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
At last the enemy could no longer stand against the Roman attacks. One group went back to the mountain, as it had done before. The other withdrew toward the baggage and wagons.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar says the battle lasted from about noon until evening, and during all that time no one could see an enemy turning his back in flight.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
The fighting continued late into the night around the baggage. The Helvetii had placed wagons as a kind of wall. From higher ground they threw weapons at the Romans as they came on. Others crouched between the wagons and wheels and thrust spears and javelins from below, wounding Roman soldiers.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
After a long struggle, Caesar's men captured the enemy baggage and camp. There they took the daughter and one of the sons of {{< name "Orgetorix" "or-GET-oh-rix" >}}.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
After the battle, about 130,000 of the enemy were still alive. They marched all through the night without stopping. On the fourth day, they reached the lands of the {{< name "Lingones" "lin-GOH-neez" >}}.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
The Romans could not follow at once. They stopped for three days because many soldiers were wounded and because they had to bury the dead.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar sent letters and messengers to the Lingones. He ordered them not to help the Helvetii with grain or anything else. If they did help them, Caesar said he would treat them the same as the Helvetii.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
After three days, Caesar began to follow the Helvetii with all his forces.
{{< /bp >}}
