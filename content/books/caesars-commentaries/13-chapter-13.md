{{< bp >}}
The {{< name "Helvetii" "hel-VEE-shee-eye" >}} now lacked almost everything. Because of this, they sent ambassadors to Caesar to talk about surrender.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
When the ambassadors met Caesar on the road, they threw themselves at his feet. With tears, they begged for peace.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar ordered them to wait where they were until he arrived. They obeyed.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
When Caesar reached the place, he demanded hostages, their weapons, and the slaves who had run away to them. These were terms of surrender. Hostages would help guarantee obedience, weapons would make rebellion harder, and returned deserters would show submission to Roman authority.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
While these things were being gathered, one group tried to escape. About six thousand men from the canton called the {{< name "Verbigeni" "ver-BIH-jeh-nee" >}} left the Helvetian camp at nightfall and hurried toward the {{< name "Rhine" "RYN" >}} and the lands of the Germans.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar gives two possible reasons. Perhaps they were afraid they would be punished after handing over their weapons. Or perhaps, in such a huge crowd of surrendered people, they hoped their flight would be hidden or simply overlooked.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
When Caesar found out, he ordered the peoples whose lands they had crossed to search for them and bring them back, if those peoples wanted Caesar to consider them innocent. When the runaways were brought back, Caesar treated them as enemies.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar accepted the surrender of all the rest after they gave up the hostages, weapons, and deserters he had demanded.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Then he ordered the Helvetii, the {{< name "Tulingi" "too-LING-ee" >}}, and the {{< name "Latobrigi" "lah-toh-BREE-gee" >}} to return to the lands they had left.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
But their homeland had been burned by their own hands before the journey began. There was nothing there to feed them. So Caesar ordered the {{< name "Allobroges" "al-LOH-broh-jeez" >}} to give them plenty of grain. He also ordered the Helvetii and their allies to rebuild the towns and villages they had burned.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar explains why he did this. He did not want the Helvetian homeland to stand empty. If it were empty, the Germans beyond the Rhine might cross over because the land was good. Then the Germans would become neighbors of the Roman Province and the Allobroges.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
The {{< name "Aedui" "EYE-dyoo-eye" >}} also asked Caesar to let the {{< name "Boii" "BOY-eye" >}} settle in Aeduan land, because the Boii were known for great courage. Caesar agreed. The Aedui gave them land and later admitted them to the same rights and freedom they themselves had.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
In the Helvetian camp, written lists were found and brought to Caesar. They were written in Greek letters. The lists counted, name by name, those who had left their country.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
The lists separated those able to bear arms from boys, old men, and women. Caesar reports the totals: 263,000 Helvetii, 36,000 Tulingi, 14,000 Latobrigi, 23,000 {{< name "Rauraci" "ROW-rah-kee" >}}, and 32,000 Boii.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Together, that made 368,000 people. Of these, about 92,000 were able to bear arms.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
When Caesar later ordered a count of those who returned home, the number was 110,000.
{{< /bp >}}
