{{< bp >}}
While the {{< name "Helvetii" "hel-VEE-shee-eye" >}} waited for Caesar's answer, Caesar did not waste the time. He had one legion with him, and more soldiers had gathered from the Roman Province.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
With these men, he built a long line of defense. It ran from {{< name "Lake Geneva" "juh-NEE-vuh" >}}, where the lake flows into the {{< name "Rhone" "ROHN" >}} River, all the way to {{< name "Mount Jura" "JOOR-uh" >}}, which separated the lands of the {{< name "Sequani" "seh-KWAH-nee" >}} from the lands of the Helvetii.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Caesar says the line was nineteen Roman miles long, which is a little less than eighteen English miles. It had a wall sixteen feet high and a trench beside it. A trench is a deep ditch dug to make crossing harder.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
When the work was finished, Caesar placed garrisons along the line. A garrison is a group of soldiers set to guard a place. He also strengthened small forts, called redoubts, so he could stop the Helvetii more easily if they tried to cross against his will.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Then the day came when the Helvetian ambassadors returned. Caesar gave his answer. He said that, according to the custom and example of the Roman people, he could not allow anyone to march through the Province. If the Helvetii tried to use force, he would oppose them.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
The Helvetii were disappointed. They had hoped to pass that way. Now they tried to force a crossing.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
Some made bridges of boats and many rafts. Others looked for shallow places in the Rhone where they could cross on foot. They tried by day, and even more often by night.
{{< /bp >}}

{{< bp >}}
But Caesar's defenses held. The wall, the trench, the gathered soldiers, and their weapons kept the Helvetii back. At last the Helvetii gave up trying to break through this road.
{{< /bp >}}
