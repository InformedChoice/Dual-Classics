Chapter 7: Five Miles Behind

Caesar follows the Helvetii closely while his army begins to run short of grain.

English
English-first version. Spanish will be added after this English version is reviewed.
English
The next day, the Helvetii hel-VEE-shee-eye moved their camp. Caesar moved his camp too.
English
He sent all his cavalry ahead. Cavalry means soldiers who fight on horseback. Caesar had about four thousand cavalry, gathered from the Roman Province, from the Aedui EYE-dyoo-eye , and from Rome’s other allies.
English
Their job was to watch where the enemy was going. But the cavalry followed the rear of the Helvetian line too eagerly. They came to a place that was bad for them and fought the Helvetian cavalry there. A few of Caesar’s men were killed.
English
The Helvetii were encouraged by this fight. They had only about five hundred horsemen, yet they had driven back a much larger number. After that, they began to face the Romans more boldly. Sometimes they even attacked from the rear, trying to provoke Caesar’s men.
English
Caesar held his soldiers back from a larger battle. For the moment, he thought it was enough to stop the Helvetii from plundering, gathering supplies freely, and harming the countryside.
English
For about fifteen days the two armies marched close to each other. The rear of the Helvetian army and the front of Caesar’s army were never more than five or six miles apart.
English
Meanwhile, Caesar kept pressing the Aedui every day for the grain they had promised in the name of their state. Grain was the army’s food supply. Without it, even a strong army could not keep marching for long.
English
There was not enough food nearby. Gaul lay far to the north, and the grain in the fields was not ripe yet. There was not even enough stored fodder, which was food for horses and other animals.
English
Caesar also could not use the grain he had brought by ships up the Saone SOHN River. The Helvetii had turned away from the river, and Caesar did not want to leave them.
English
The Aedui kept delaying. One day they said the grain was being collected. Another day they said it was being brought in. Another day they said it was on the road.
English
At last Caesar saw that they had delayed too long. The day was near when he needed to give grain to his soldiers. So he called together many of the Aeduan chiefs who were in his camp.
English
Among them were Divitiacus dih-vih-tee-AH-kus and Liscus LIS-kus . Liscus held the highest yearly office among the Aedui. The Aedui called this officer the Vergobretus ver-goh-BREH-tus . He had power of life and death over his own people.
English
Caesar rebuked them sharply. The enemy was close. Grain could not be bought, and it could not yet be gathered from the fields. The Aedui themselves had begged Caesar to take up the war, yet now they were not helping him in an urgent need.
English
For that reason, Caesar complained all the more bitterly that he had been left without the help he had been promised.
